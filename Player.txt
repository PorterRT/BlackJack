Blackjack Objective:
The primary goal is to have a hand value that is closer to 21 than the dealer's hand without going over 21.

Card Values:
Number cards (2-10): Worth their face value.
Face cards (Kings, Queens, Jacks): Worth 10 points.
Aces: Worth 1 or 11 points, depending on which value benefits the hand more.

Rules:
Beginning of the Game: 
 
Each player, including the dealer, is dealt two cards. One of the dealer's cards is face-up, and the other is face-down.

Player's Turn: 
 
Players decide how to play their hands:
Hit: Take another card.
Stand: Keep the current total.
Double Down: Double the initial bet in exchange for one more card (and only one).
Split: If the first two cards are of the same value, a player can split them into two separate hands. An additional bet, equal to the initial bet, is placed for the second hand. Each hand is then played separately.

Dealer's Turn: 
 
After all players have finished, the dealer reveals their hidden card.
The dealer must hit until their hand totals 17 or more points. Some casinos specify that a dealer must also hit on a soft 17 (a hand with an Ace valued as 11, e.g., Ace and 6).

Winning the Game:
If a player's hand exceeds 21, it's a "bust," and they lose their bet for that hand.
If the dealer busts, all remaining players win their bets.
If neither busts, the hand closest to 21 without exceeding it wins.
A Blackjack (an Ace and a 10-value card as the initial two cards) pays 3:2 usually, unless the dealer also has Blackjack. In the case where both the player and dealer have Blackjack, the hand is a "push" (a tie).

Insurance:
If the dealer's face-up card is an Ace, players can take "insurance," a side bet that the dealer has a 10-value card (and thus a Blackjack) as the hidden card.
The insurance bet pays 2:1 if the dealer has Blackjack. If not, the insurance bet is lost.

Push (Tie):
If the player and the dealer have the same hand value, the hand is a "push," and the player's bet is returned.

Payouts:
Winning hands usually pay even money (1:1).
Blackjack (an Ace and a 10-value card) usually pays 3:2, though this can vary in some casinos.
Insurance pays 2:1.


CARD COUNTING:
Gauge the nature of the remaining deck: rich in high cards (10s, face cards, and Aces) or low cards.
A deck rich in high cards is advantageous to the player, while a deck rich in low cards is advantageous to the dealer.

Basic Card Counting System - The Hi-Lo System:
Assign a Point Value:
2 to 6: +1
7 to 9: 0
10, face cards, and Aces: -1

Running Count:
Start with a count of 0 at the beginning of the deck or shoe.
As cards are revealed, add or subtract based on the point value. This sum is the running count.

True Count:
To account for multiple decks (since most casinos use multiple decks), you need to convert the running count into the true count.
True Count = Running Count / Number of Decks Remaining.
It's an estimate, but with practice, you can gauge how many decks remain in the shoe.

Change Your Bets:
When the true count is positive (indicating the deck is rich in high cards), increase your bets.
When the true count is negative (indicating the deck is rich in low cards), decrease your bets.

Advanced Techniques and Systems:
Side Counts: Some players keep a separate count of specific cards, like Aces.
Different Systems: There are various counting systems, like the Omega II, Hi-Opt I, and Hi-Opt II, each with its own set of values and challenges.
Deck Penetration: It refers to the percentage of cards played before a reshuffle. The deeper the penetration, the more effective card counting can be.
